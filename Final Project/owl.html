OWL

Owls are birds from the order Strigiformes, which includes over 200 species of mostly solitary and nocturnal birds of prey typified by an upright stance, a large, broad head, binocular vision, binaural hearing, sharp talons, and feathers adapted for silent flight. Exceptions include the diurnal northern hawk-owl and the gregarious burrowing owl. To scale if humans had eyes as large as owls, they would be big as oranges.

Owls hunt mostly small mammals, insects, and other birds, although a few species specialize in hunting fish. Nineteen owl species are found in the United States. Ranging from the diminutive Elf Owl — the smallest owl on the continent — to the massive Great Gray Owl, these charismatic birds come in many shapes and sizes.They are found in all regions of the Earth except the polar ice caps and some remote islands. Owls have the same kind of feet as woodpeckers, two toes in front, and two in back.

Owls are divided into two families: the true (or typical) owl family, Strigidae, and the barn-owl family, Tytonidae. While owls' extraordinary hunting skills, nocturnal habits, and haunting calls are the stuff of legend, the dangers they face are often overlooked. Threats like habitat loss, pesticides, and vehicle collisions have already sent a third of all owl species in the United States into decline.
